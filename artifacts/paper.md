# Conventional superconductivity at 203 kelvin at high pressures in the sulfur hydride system

**Authors:** A.P. Drozdov, M.I. Eremets, I.A. Troyan, V. Ksenofontov, S.I. Shylin

**Published:** Nature **525**, 73–76 (2015). DOI: [10.1038/nature14964](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature14964)

**arXiv:** [1412.0460](https://arxiv.org/abs/1412.0460) [cond-mat.supr-con], submitted 1 Dec 2014

**Note:** The arXiv preprint (v1) reports Tc up to ~190 K. The published Nature version reports 203 K based on additional magnetic susceptibility measurements.

---

## Abstract

The highest critical temperature of superconductivity Tc has been achieved in cuprates: 133 K at ambient pressure and 164 K at high pressures. As the nature of superconductivity in these materials is still not disclosed, the prospects for a higher Tc are not clear. In contrast the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) theory gives a clear guide for achieving high Tc: it should be a favorable combination of high frequency phonons, strong coupling between electrons and phonons, and high density of states. These conditions can be fulfilled for metallic hydrogen and covalent hydrogen dominant compounds. Numerous calculations supported this idea and predicted Tc = 100–235 K for many hydrides but only moderate Tc ~ 17 K has been observed experimentally. Here we found that sulfur hydride transforms at P ~ 90 GPa to metal and superconductor with Tc increasing with pressure to 150 K at ~200 GPa. This is in general agreement with recent calculations of Tc ~ 80 K for H₂S. Moreover we found superconductivity with Tc ~ 190 K in a H₂S sample pressurized to P > 150 GPa at T > 220 K. This superconductivity likely associates with the dissociation of H₂S, and formation of SHₙ (n > 2) hydrides. We proved occurrence of superconductivity by the drop of the resistivity at least 50 times lower than the copper resistivity, the decrease of Tc with magnetic field, and the strong isotope shift of Tc in D₂S which evidences a major role of phonons in the superconductivity.

## 1. Introduction

A room temperature superconductor is "the most desired system in solid state physics." The greatest advances — cuprates, pnictides — were obtained serendipitously. As there is no clear theory for these unconventional superconductors, progress is hard to predict. The BCS theory (Eliashberg formulation) puts no apparent bounds on Tc. Materials with light elements are favorable (high phonon frequencies). The highest Tc from conventional search was 39 K in MgB₂. Common preconception: Tc ~ 30 K is the maximum for conventional superconductors.

### Ashcroft's hydrogen hypothesis

N. Ashcroft (1968, Ref 4) predicted metallic hydrogen would be a high-Tc superconductor:
- Very high vibrational frequencies due to light hydrogen atom
- Strong electron-phonon interaction
- Covalent bonding

Further calculations: Tc ~ 100–240 K for molecular hydrogen, Tc = 300–350 K for atomic phase at 500 GPa. Superconductivity in pure hydrogen has not yet been found. Hydrogen-dominant covalent hydrides (SiH₄, SnH₄, etc.) are alternative candidates at lower pressures.

### Theoretical predictions for H₂S

Li et al. (2014, Ref 7) predicted:
- H₂S metallizes at ~96 GPa
- New stable structures at P > 130 GPa
- Tc ~ 80 K at 160 GPa

Phase diagram complications:
- Partial dissociation of H₂S at P > 27 GPa (room temperature)
- Elemental sulfur becomes metallic above 95 GPa
- Li et al. found precipitation of sulfur to be "very unlikely"

### Why H₂S was chosen

- Relatively easy to handle
- Transforms to metal at low pressure (~100 GPa)
- High predicted Tc = 80 K

## 2. Experimental Results

### 2.1 H₂S metallization

- H₂S starts to conduct at P ~ 50 GPa (semiconductor: photoconductivity observed)
- At 90–100 GPa: metallic temperature dependence, no photoconductivity
- H₂S is a poor metal: ρ ~ 3×10⁻⁵ Ω·m at 110 GPa, ρ ~ 3×10⁻⁷ Ω·m at ~200 GPa

### 2.2 Low-temperature route (Fig. 2a)

- Cooling to 4 K at ~100 GPa: resistance drops 3–4 orders of magnitude at Tc ~ 23 K
- Tc increases with pressure, sharp growth approaching 200 GPa
- Tc values: 23 K (100 GPa) → rising to ~150 K (~200 GPa)
- Pressure applied at 100–150 K range
- Reasonably agrees with Li et al. calculations for H₂S (predicted Tc ~ 80 K)

### 2.3 High-temperature route — THE KEY DISCOVERY (Fig. 2b)

- A second superconducting state with Tc ~ 190 K found by applying P > 150 GPa at T > 220 K
- Weak pressure dependence (very different from low-T route)
- Often accompanied by a step at Tc ~ 30 K that disappears with time (~1 day) or further pressure
- Oscillations on R(T) with period 25–30 K seen in multiple runs
- NOT predicted by H₂S calculations → decomposition of H₂S involved

## 3. Three Proofs of Superconductivity

### 3.1 Resistivity drop

Minimum resistance: at least ~10⁻¹¹ Ω·m — about two orders of magnitude less than pure copper at the same temperature. Resistivity at 144 K: 1.7×10⁻¹⁰ Ω·m — 50 times lower than copper (at 150 K: ρ_Cu = 70×10⁻¹⁰ Ω·m).

### 3.2 Magnetic field dependence

Tc shifts to lower temperatures with magnetic field up to 7 Tesla. Extrapolation of Tc(B) using Hc(T) = Hc0(1−(T/Tc)²):
- Estimated Hc2 ~ 25 T for Tc ~ 60 K state
- Estimated Hc2 ~ 70 T for Tc ~ 185 K state

### 3.3 Isotope effect (D₂S)

BCS theory: Tc ~ M^(-α) where α_H ~ 0.5. Observed:
- H₂S Tc ~ 60 K → D₂S Tc ~ 30 K
- H₂S Tc ~ 185 K → D₂S Tc ~ 90 K
- This confirms phonon-assisted (conventional BCS) superconductivity

## 4. Interpretation: H₃S Formation

### 4.1 Ruling out alternative explanations

- Elemental sulfur superconductivity too low to explain observations (Fig. 2a)
- No H₂ vibron observed in Raman → simple H₂S → H₂ + S decomposition ruled out
- Li et al. calculations: H₂S → H₂ + S is "energetically very unfavorable"

### 4.2 Proposed H₃S formation mechanism

Proposed reactions:
- 2H₂S → H₄S + S
- 3H₂S → H₆S + S

These reactions are plausible because sulfur has valency 2, 4, and 6. H₄S and H₆S are thermodynamically unstable but kinetically stable at ambient pressure; high pressure might stabilize them.

### 4.3 Theoretical support (Duan et al., Ref 9)

Duan et al. (2014) studied the van der Waals compound (H₂S)₂H₂:
- Above 111 GPa: H₃S molecular unit is built
- Above 180 GPa: sulfur coordination number reaches 6
- Predicted Tc ~ 160 K and ~190 K for the two phases
- Close to experimental values

Raman measurements of the sample with Tc ~ 190 K showed a phase transformation at 180 GPa, consistent with Duan et al.

---

## 5. Implications

H₂S has a moderate hydrogen content. High Tc can be expected in a wide range of hydrogen-containing materials:
- Carbon-based: fullerenes, aromatic hydrocarbons, graphanes
- Could be turned to superconducting state by doping or gating instead of pressure
- Hydrogen atoms essential for high-frequency phonon modes and strong electron-phonon coupling
